Layer 06 — language

A character is a number in costume

Every character you type is already a number. Type one below and watch it undress: code point, bits, and the literal voltage waveform that carries it down a wire — eight bits, high for one, low for zero.

A → 65 →

High ≈ supply voltage, low ≈ 0 V. Eight wires in parallel, or eight ticks on one wire, carry this byte through the machine.

Layer 05 — compilation

Eight bytes is a whole function

Layer 04 — decode

The moment software becomes hardware

A fetched instruction lands in a register. Its opcode bits drive a decoder; the decoder raises exactly one control line; and that line physically selects which circuit inside the ALU the operand pulses flow through. Change the instruction — watch a different wire light and a different answer come back. Here w0 = 12 and w1 = 10.

Only the amber opcode field changes between these four instructions — the register fields stay put.

One decoded line per operation is a simplification — a real core decodes to dozens of control signals (register selects, write enables, memory strobes) — but the principle is exactly this: bits choosing wires, wires choosing circuits.

Layer 03 — the die

Signals climb a copper skyline

Zooming out: billions of those decoded pulses race through a real die. One thin layer of transistors sits at the bottom — the only layer that computes — with a dozen-plus layers of copper wiring stacked above it, joined by vertical vias. Drag the clock. Click a layer.

Click a layer. The amber pulse is one signal: fired at a transistor gate, hopping gate to gate, then climbing via to via through the wiring stack toward the package.

Real chips alternate routing direction between metal layers, like plywood grain — you can see it in the trace directions here.

Layer 02 — the switch

No electron ever enters the gate

At the bottom of the stack, the only moving part is an electric field. Voltage on the gate reaches through an insulating oxide and pulls electrons up into a channel between source and drain. Remove the voltage and the bridge dissolves. The switch is a field effect — that's the F in MOSFET.

Channel closed — no current

Layer 00 — electrons. Below this there is no machinery left: only charge, fields, and the arrangement we impose on them.

Interlude — two philosophies

One fast stream, or ten thousand in lockstep

Same transistors, opposite bets. A CPU spends its silicon making one stream of instructions finish as fast as physics allows. A GPU spends it running one instruction across thousands of data lanes at once — same operation, different data — which is why matrices and hash workloads love it.

CPU — latency machine

The gray bars are register banks — results wait there for the next clock tick before entering the next stage. Branch prediction and big caches exist to keep this one stream from ever stalling.

GPU — throughput machine

Each cell is a small core. The bright wave is a single instruction executing across all of them in lockstep (SIMT) — not all instructions at once, but one instruction over thousands of operands at once.

Margins — field notes

Notes from the fab

13.5 nm

The wavelength chips are printed with — extreme-ultraviolet light made by lasering molten tin droplets 50,000 times a second.

100 B+

Transistors on a flagship die — roughly as many switches as there are stars in the galaxy, on a fingernail of silicon.

~20 nm

Width of key features. A virus is about five times bigger; a red blood cell is a continent by comparison.

6 cm

How far light travels in one 5 GHz clock cycle. On a 2 cm die, wire length is destiny — layout is applied relativity.

~1 ns

An L1 cache answer. Main RAM takes ~80 ns — the entire cache hierarchy exists to hide that gap from your one fast stream.

$20 B

Cost of a leading-edge fab. Each wafer spends about three months inside, passing through more than a thousand steps.

end of descent

Text becomes numbers, numbers become instructions, instructions become fields, and fields move electrons. Everything above layer zero is arrangement.
